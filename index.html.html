Mathematical Logic

Statements or Propositions


A Declarative Sentence that can either be true or false but not both simultaneously is reffered as a statement or propositions.

for ex - 'One plus three equals four' is a statement


Questions, commands, and exclamations are not statements.
The simple propositions are represented by the letters p, q, r, s etc. which are known as propositions varibales.


some examples :

(i) Where are you going - this is a statement (because it is a question)

(ii) 13 is an even number - this is a statement (because it is false)


Compound Proposition and Connectives


A Compound proposition, also known as a Compound statement, is a logical statement formed by combining two or more simpler propositions, using logical operators.


Connectives are logical operators that are used to combine or modify propositions to form compound statements.


Types of Connectives


The most commonly used Connectives include :


Conjunction (AND) :

It is denoted by the symbol "∧". It is true only when both component propositions are true.


Disjunction (OR) :

It is denoted by the symbol "∨". It is true when at least one of the component propositions is true.


Negation (NOT) :

It is denoted by the symbol "∼". It negates the truth value of a proposition, turning true into false and false into true.


Implication (IF - THEN) :

It is denoted by the symbol "→" or "⇒".

It expresses a conditional relationship between two propositions. It is false only when the antecedent (if-part) is true and the consequent (then-part) is false.


Biconditional (IF AND ONLY IF) :

It is denoted by the symbol "↔" or sometimes "⇔".

It indicated that two propositions are true or false together.

It is true when both propositions have the same truth value.


Truth Table


Truth tables are a methodological technique to express the potential truth values of compound statements depending on the potential truth values of their component propositions


They provide a clear and concise method to analyse and evaluate the truth values of complex logical expressoions.


To construct a truth table, follow these steps :

Identify the simple propositions involved in the compound propositions.

Determine the number of rows needed in the truth table based on the number of simple propositions. For n component propositions, there will be 2 n rows.

Assign truth values (true or False) to the simple propositions for each row in a systematic manner.

Evaluate the truth value of the compound proposition based on the truth values assigned to the component propositions
